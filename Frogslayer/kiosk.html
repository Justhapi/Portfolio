Overview

The problem with kiosks today

Frogslayer's clients were increasingly adopting kiosks — but every deployment was a one-off. No shared patterns, no scalable foundation. The result: user frustration and a product team rebuilding the same components over and over.

"Build a kiosk guideline identity — a framework that works now and accommodates future teams who will expand it."

01 — Research

Understanding kiosks from the ground up

Research ran in two parallel tracks: a sponsor content audit to align with Frogslayer's existing work, and secondary research across four areas.

Key Insights

  • Inconsistent element designs — critical for reducing cognitive load
  • Vague affordances — simplify to reduce user hesitation
  • Overloaded pages — reduce complexity to decrease time-on-page friction
  • Hidden loyalty programs — integrate into the natural user flow, not as detours

02 — Design

From findings to frameworks

Research insights became four design principles, then guided the construction of a full arcade kiosk user flow (Litally) — chosen because its complexity was a strong stress test for the guidelines.

Clarity over cleverness

Every element communicates its function immediately. Affordances must be explicit, not implied.

Reduce cognitive load

Limit decisions per screen. Group related elements and establish clear visual hierarchy.

Flow-integrated discovery

Loyalty features appear naturally within the journey — not as detours.

Scalable consistency

Components must be documented well enough for future teams to extend without breaking coherence.

03 — Testing

Verifying assumptions with real users

Moderated usability sessions with first-time and returning kiosk users. Think-aloud protocols surfaced reasoning alongside behavior.

04 — Reflections

What I took away

What worked

Grounding decisions in research gave the team a shared justification to return to when disagreements arose — and made sponsor presentations significantly more confident.

What I'd change

A dedicated synthesis session before prototyping would have prevented some design directions from being harder to pivot than they should have been.

On systems thinking

Designing a guidelines document shifts your thinking — every decision needs to generalize to contexts you haven't imagined yet.

On leading

Clarity of task ownership mattered as much as design quality. Managing a 6-person team across an external sponsor is its own discipline.
